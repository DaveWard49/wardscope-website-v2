What does "resolution" actually mean?

Resolution is how finely WardScope can separate two close frequencies. It's set by two numbers: how many points your FFT uses, and how fast your audio is sampled.

FFT Size

More points means finer detail within the same sample rate — at the cost of more processing per update.

Sample Rate

A higher sample rate raises the highest frequency you can see before it folds back as an alias.

A note on your hardware

Higher tiers unlock larger FFT sizes and sample rates in the software — but what you actually capture is limited by your audio interface, not just your CPU. To get the full benefit of PC 3Hz's 192 kHz ceiling, you'll need an audio interface that genuinely supports 192 kHz capture; otherwise the software may display a finer-looking trace than your hardware can really deliver.

The good news: you don't need an expensive interface to get there. Audio interfaces that support 192 kHz capture are commonly available from around $200 — that's enough for most users. Interfaces priced well beyond that (some run into the thousands) are paying for extra channels and preamp quality, not for 192 kHz support itself.

Why "more resolution" can lie to you

Every sample rate has a ceiling, called the Nyquist frequency — half the sample rate. Sound above that ceiling can't be represented correctly. Instead of vanishing, it folds back down and shows up as a false, lower frequency that was never actually there. This is called aliasing.

Good audio hardware filters out that excess content before it can fold back — this is called anti-aliasing filtering. Cheap or poorly-implemented hardware skips or skimps on this step.

That matters here specifically: a sound card can technically claim 192 kHz support while doing a poor job of the filtering underneath. WardScope will still show you a fine, 3 Hz-resolution trace — but if the hardware behind it is letting aliased content through, part of what you're looking at may be a false signal, not a real one. A finer-looking trace is only meaningful if the hardware capturing it is honest.

Sound cards vary enormously in quality, and price doesn't track perfectly with how well a card actually handles this — a clean ~$200 interface can be more trustworthy at 192 kHz than a far more expensive one bought for other reasons (more channels, mic preamps) rather than for sampling accuracy. For most users, this is the real bang-for-buck decision: matching your tier to a sound card that's actually honest at that sample rate matters more than chasing the highest-priced gear.

In short: buying a higher software tier always gets you a finer display. Whether that finer display reflects your actual sound, or partly reflects aliasing artifacts, depends on the quality of your audio interface — not on WardScope.
